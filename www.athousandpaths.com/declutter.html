Is “too much” bogging you down? 

stuff • stress • on your schedule  

In this two-hour experiential group, we’ll gently explore the parts that protect through doing too much and keeping too much.

Begin the journey of simplifying and letting go by joining Melissa Sandfort in this supportive, practical workshop!

Details:

I grew up with a family legacy gift/burden of loving a sale!

My great-grandmother owned an antique store; my mother used to bring me to the Scranton flea market on the weekends. I grew up bringing home treasures from garage sales and the Columbia Mall flea market. 

By my mid 20s, my house was full of stuff!

I went through periodic declutters, but never got serious until Covid hit. Stuck in my house with all my stuff, I was overwhelmed. 

In waves and with all kinds of support, I began to dig out in earnest.

I watched all the decluttering series on Netflix—The Home Edit and Marie Kondo— as well as reading both of Kondo books. I also spent dozens of hours watching minimalist YouTube videos— my favorite is Dawn from The Minimal Mom. I also participated extensively in a supportive minimalism Facebook group. 

With this encouragement, role modeling, and targeted information, I was able to move through a lot of my excess inventory. 

IFS contributed about 50% to these phases— I would talk to parts about their fears or concerns regarding letting certain things go, and move through the letting-go process in relationship with them.

This last round of decluttering was different. It was led by one of my parts, Captain Arrr (obviously a pirate), who believed we were not decluttering deeply enough. 

Despite a rocky start, his vision of a radically decluttered house moved us through five solid months of endless cutting that pared my house down to the essentials. 

I am overjoyed to be on the other side of the process! It was an embodied learning experience about what it takes to declutter to the point where your house can breathe – where cabinets are only 1/2 to 2/3 full, where everything has a place, and everything earns its keep. 

This was a Self-led declutter, and probably 90% of the time I was doing IFS with every object and every area as I cut, cut, cut. All parts weighed in, and heated discussions were had over many an object. There was a particular skirmish related to pot lids! 

Now that I’m living the dream of minimalism, I want to share what I learned in the process of this IFS declutter. 

I’m looking forward to tailoring the class to meet the needs of each person, whether you’re struggling with excess stuff or excess commitments on the calendar. 

See you in June!

Class Safety Agreement

Clarity about our purpose: 

To deepen our capacity to do IFS and to develop our Self-leadership as human beings.

Privacy and Confidentiality:
These classes require a private, confidential space. A private room in your home or office. Or a private conference room in a library or your car parked in a quiet space. This is for the safety of all participants, as we can not open and focus inside if we are distracted by external environments.

If you join the Zoom from a public place, like a cafe, store or park, you will be asked to leave the group.

Zoom Video On:
Being able to see everyone in the group enhances safety, so plan to have your video on during most of the class, which is not recorded. Turning video off during personal practice time is fine.

Choice: 
You are free to censor what you disclose. No is a complete sentence. Choose to share ONLY what you feel comfortable with.

Confidentiality: 
All material everyone shares should not be shared with people who are not members of this group, even if you don’t use their name. You’d be surprised how far stories can travel. To read more, visit https://www.athousandpaths.com/safety-story.

Speak up for Safety: 
If you are having difficulty with something that’s triggered you, PLEASE speak for your parts, either directly in the group or as soon as you can afterwards. Please LISTEN to your parts when they tell you what they need in order to feel safe. Safety is our number one value. When you speak for your safety needs, you speak for the safety needs of the whole group. 

Speaking for parts, not from parts: 
To the extent that you can, speaking for parts, not from them, will help us maintain safety when sharing in the group. 

Embodying parts: 
During coaching demos, in distinction to other times during the group, feel free to speak FROM parts and to experiment with embodying them. The difference between blending and embodying is: when you are blended, you are in a part and you don’t know you are in a part. When you embody a part, you are in a part and YOU and the part BOTH know you are ‘being’ the part. 

No Coercion: 
"Thank you, for letting me, be myself!” In this group, there is “no crosstalk.” This means when one person shares, no one judges, gives advice, or reacts against what they shared.

Feedback:  Share how you are inspired or how you resonate with others. DO NOT share judgement, advice, or criticism. If you criticize others in the class, I reserve the right to determine that you may not be the right fit for my classes. Thank you for understanding. 


https://radicalifs.substack.com offers endless examples of how parts crosstalk and then repair, if you’re interested in a deeper dive. 


Timeliness: 
We commit to start and end on time. We start on time; this increases safety and strengthens the container. 

Support and responsibility: 
Ultimately, the only person who can keep you fully safe in a group is you. I want to do everything I can to make this group a safe space to learn, to go inside, and to share, in whatever ways feel right for you — but you are responsible for listening to the sometimes difficult-to-hear voices who tell you what they REALLY need to be safe. 


You are not crazy if you get triggered, it’s ULTRA NORMAL in IFS work. So take care of yourself, and possibly give yourself extra support on days when you do IFS!

Thank you for your commitment to this safety agreement!
